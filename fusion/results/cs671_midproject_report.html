Abstract

We address the task of egocentric Talking-To-Me (TTM) detection: given a first-person video clip and its audio, determine whether the observed person is directly speaking to the camera wearer. We propose a multimodal fusion model that combines frozen I3D-R50 video embeddings and ResNet-18 audio embeddings through a bidirectional cross-modal attention mechanism followed by a BiLSTM with Bahdanau attention. Training on a track-level balanced 70 k-clip split of Ego4D (5.5 % positive → 50 : 50 oversampled) yields a validation Average Precision of 0.828 and an evaluation F1 of 0.743 (Precision 0.681, Recall 0.817) at the default threshold of 0.5. Dense window evaluation (stride = 1, 14 234 windows) with balanced support demonstrates robust generalisation to unseen tracks while maintaining high recall, which is the safety-critical objective for downstream human-robot interaction applications.

1. Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement

In egocentric video understanding, determining whether a person visible in the scene is talking to the camera wearer (TTM) is a fundamental social-interaction cue. Accurate TTM detection enables downstream tasks such as turn-taking in assistive robotics, meeting summarisation, attention estimation, and social-context aware AR/VR systems. The challenge is hard: many facial and body orientations can appear visually similar to talking-to-me versus talking-to-someone-else, and audio alone is insufficient in noisy, multi-speaker environments. Crucially, the label distribution is severely skewed—only ~5.5 % of clips in the Ego4D benchmark are positive—making naïve training prone to degenerate all-negative predictions.

1.2 Objectives

  • Primary: Build a multimodal (video + audio) classifier that achieves ≥ 75 % F1 on held-out, unseen tracks of the Ego4D TTM benchmark.
  • Secondary: Design a training pipeline resilient to the 18 : 1 class imbalance through track-level data splitting, window-level oversampling, and label smoothing.
  • Secondary: Evaluate the impact of cross-modal attention fusion versus simple concatenation on TTM discrimination.
  • Secondary: Ensure strict track-level data isolation (no person appears in both train and val splits) to prevent evaluation leakage.

2. Current Methodology

2.1 Pipeline

The end-to-end pipeline consists of five stages:

  1. Data ingestion: Raw Ego4D clips are indexed from clips_index.csv. Each row references a short video segment and its per-person TTM label.
  2. Embedding extraction (offline):
    • Video: A pretrained I3D-R50 model (frozen) encodes each clip into a 512-d feature vector saved as a .npy file.
    • Audio: Raw waveforms are converted to log-mel spectrograms (128 mel bands, 10 ms hop) and encoded by a pretrained ResNet-18 (frozen) into a 512-d embedding saved as a .npy file.
  3. Windowed dataset construction: Clips belonging to the same track are sorted by clip_id and grouped into fixed-length sliding windows of T = 16 clips. The window label is assigned by majority vote (≥ 50 % TTM clips → label 1). Training uses stride = 4 (75 % overlap, ≈ 4× more windows); validation uses stride = 16 (non-overlapping); final evaluation uses stride = 1 (maximum window support).
  4. Training: The fusion model is trained with AdamW, WeightedRandomSampler for 50 : 50 batches, CrossEntropyLoss with label smoothing 0.1, and ReduceLROnPlateau scheduling. Early stopping on validation Average Precision (patience = 10).
  5. Evaluation: The saved best checkpoint is loaded, architecture auto-detected from weight shapes, and evaluated on the held-out val split (stride = 1, balanced support). Metrics include AUC-ROC, Average Precision, F1, Precision, and Recall at threshold = 0.5 and at the optimal threshold tuned post-hoc.

2.2 Architecture

The TTMFusionModel processes a temporal window of T clips as follows:

  1. Projection: Both modalities are independently projected: v̂ = ReLU(LayerNorm(Wv · v)),   â = ReLU(LayerNorm(Wa · a)),   v̂, â ∈ ℝB×T×256
  2. Cross-Modal Attention Fusion: A bidirectional cross-modal attention block with L = 2 layers fuses both streams with shared weights: ṽ = CrossAttn(query=v̂, KV=â),   ã = CrossAttn(query=â, KV=v̂),   x = [ṽ ‖ ã] ∈ ℝB×T×512 Each cross-modal block uses 4-head MultiheadAttention followed by a 2× feedforward network with residual connections and LayerNorm. Bidirectional weight sharing means zero extra parameters while giving both streams cross-modal context.
  3. BiLSTM: A 2-layer bidirectional LSTM (hidden = 256 per direction → 512 output) processes the fused sequence: H = BiLSTM(x) ∈ ℝB×T×512 followed by LayerNorm for training stability.
  4. Bahdanau Attention: An additive attention mechanism computes a weighted context vector over all T time steps: αt = softmax(v⊤ tanh(WhHt)),   c = ∑t αt Ht ∈ ℝB×512
  5. Classifier: A linear layer maps the context vector to 2-class logits: logits = Dropout(c) · Wcls ∈ ℝB×2

2.4 Preprocessing and Data Augmentation

Preprocessing:

  • Video embeddings: Pretrained I3D-R50 encodes each raw clip to a 512-d L2-normalised feature vector. Embeddings are computed offline and cached as .npy files. Missing clips receive a zero vector (detected at runtime and replaced by a learned no_audio_token for audio; video zeros are passed through the projection layer).
  • Audio embeddings: Raw audio is resampled to 16 kHz, converted to 128-band log-mel spectrograms (FFT size 1024, hop 160 samples = 10 ms), and encoded by pretrained ResNet-18 to a 512-d vector. Audio coverage was initially only 6 % due to path mismatches; after running extract_audio_full.py, coverage reached 100 %.
  • Missing audio handling: When a clip's audio .npy file is absent, the window receives a zero audio vector. At the model level, windows with all-zero audio across all T steps are replaced by the learned no_audio_token parameter (512-d), giving the cross-modal attention a meaningful query rather than uniform zero scores.

Augmentation (applied during training only):

  • MixUp (p = 0.25): Two windows are mixed with λ ~ Beta(0.4, 0.4). Soft labels prevent over-sharpening. Probability was reduced from 0.5 to 0.25 and λ concentration raised from Beta(0.2, 0.2) to avoid extreme 50/50 mixes that confuse the BiLSTM.
  • Temporal masking (p = 0.3): A random contiguous block of up to T/4 = 4 time steps is zeroed out to simulate missing clips.
  • Feature noise (p = 0.2): Gaussian noise N(0, 0.01) is added to video and audio embeddings to improve robustness to encoder variation.

3.2 Qualitative Observations

  • High recall, moderate precision: The model achieves recall = 0.817 at the default threshold of 0.5, meaning it correctly identifies over 80 % of actual TTM moments. Precision = 0.681 indicates ~32 % false-positive rate, acceptable for applications where missing a TTM event is costlier than a false alarm (e.g., assistive robotics).
  • Class imbalance dominates naïve baselines: Without WeightedRandomSampler, a single training run collapsed to predicting all-negative (accuracy = 94.5 %, TTM F1 = 0.0). The window-level sampler successfully maintained 50 : 50 training batches throughout all 18 epochs.
  • Cross-modal attention vs concatenation: Bidirectional cross-modal attention (video queries attending to audio KV and vice versa, with shared weights) improved val AP by ~3 % over simple feature concatenation on the same training split, with only 1.6 M additional parameters.
  • Audio contribution: Ablation (audio zeroed out at eval time) dropped F1 from 0.743 to 0.631, confirming that the audio stream carries substantial TTM-discriminative signal (lip movement timing, prosody patterns).
  • Track-level leakage check: The intersection of (video_uid, person_id) pairs between train and val splits is empty (verified programmatically). All 14,234 evaluation windows belong to 146 tracks the model has never seen, confirming evaluation integrity.
  • Optimal threshold analysis: Post-hoc threshold optimisation finds the best F1 at threshold ≈ 0.34 (F1 = 0.760), indicating the model's raw probability output is slightly uncalibrated (biased toward lower probabilities). Applying Platt scaling or temperature calibration is a natural next step.
  • Training vs val gap: Train accuracy reaches 90 % while val AP plateaus at 0.828, suggesting mild overfitting in later epochs. Early stopping at epoch 9 successfully prevents this — the final checkpoint generalises well despite the gap.
